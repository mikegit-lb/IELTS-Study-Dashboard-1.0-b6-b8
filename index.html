Progress is not linear. Early gains come quickly from learning the exam. Later gains require genuine language growth and feel slower. That plateau around Band 6 is normal — it's the gains going underground before they show, not failure.

🇹🇷 Turkish-L1 reality check: Articles (a/the), the passive voice, and complex tense control are the slowest-moving features for Turkish speakers — Turkish has no article system, so your brain naturally skips them. You can learn the rule in one lesson, but accuracy under exam pressure takes weeks of spaced practice. This is normal and completely trainable. We never treat "fix articles" as one-and-done.

The four ground rules of this system

1 · Doable beats ambitious

One new strategy, one grammar point, and 5–7 new words per study block — no more. Homework is sized so you'll actually finish it. A small habit you keep beats a big plan you abandon.

2 · Sequence by impact

For a Band 5 writer/speaker, we fix Task Response, coherence and grammatical accuracy first — the things capping your score — before polishing fancy vocabulary.

3 · Retrieve, don't re-read

You learn by producing from memory (mini-quizzes, gap-fills, speaking aloud), spaced across days — not by re-reading notes. Re-reading feels productive but barely sticks.

4 · Protect the loop

Bank small wins early. Track sub-skills, not just the headline band. Feeling that you're improving is what keeps you studying — and studying is what makes you improve.

Study Notes — All Five Sections

Core knowledge for each part of the exam, anchored to what moves you from Band 5 toward Band 7.

Listening 40 Q · ~30 min · played ONCE

SectionSettingDifficulty
1Social/transactional (booking, enquiry) — 2 speakersEasiest
2Informational monologue (tour, talk) — 1 speakerEasy–Medium
3Academic discussion (tutorial) — 2–4 speakersMedium–Hard
4Academic lecture — 1 speakerHardest

The three things that lift your Listening band

  • Predict before the audio. In the reading time, read every gap and decide the word type: number? name? place? day? Your brain then catches it faster.
  • Listen for the idea, not the word (parallel expression). The audio almost never repeats the question's words. "It's not very expensive" → answer is affordable.
  • Section 4 = follow the signposts. "Firstly… moving on to… in contrast… to summarise" mark topic shifts. Ride the structure and never leave a gap blank.
Mike says: "When they say '30 seconds to read', use every second. Don't wait for the audio — build a mental map of what's coming. Prediction is half the score."
🇹🇷 Watch for: mishearing final consonants ("bed/bet"), and number confusion — thirty vs thirteen, 15th vs 50th. Drill these specifically; they cost easy marks.

Reading 40 Q · 60 min · no transfer time

PassageTimeNote
117 minFactual, concrete — bank time here
220 minMore abstract
323 minHardest — allow more

Skim → Scan → Read

  1. Skim (2 min): title, first sentence of each paragraph, last paragraph. Know where topics live.
  2. Read the questions, underline keywords, predict which paragraph holds each answer.
  3. Scan that paragraph only — hunt for paraphrases, not identical words.
  4. 90-second rule: stuck? Circle it, move on. 40 questions, equal marks — never lose three easy ones chasing one hard one.

The classic trap

NOT GIVEN ≠ FALSE. FALSE = the passage directly contradicts the statement. NOT GIVEN = the passage is silent on it. And TFNG = facts; YES/NO/NG = the writer's opinion.

🇹🇷 Watch for: picking FALSE when the answer is NOT GIVEN (Turkish texts tend to be explicit, so silence feels wrong), and over-translating word-by-word, which kills your speed.

Writing Task 1 20 min · 150+ words (aim 170–190)

Visuals: line graph · bar chart · pie chart · table · process diagram · map. Criteria: TA · CC · LR · GRA. Worth 1/3 of Writing.

The four-part shape

  1. Introduction — paraphrase the prompt (change 60%+ of the words; never copy).
  2. Overview — the big picture, no numbers. This separates Band 6 from Band 7
  3. Body 1 — the most significant / largest data with figures.
  4. Body 2 — key trends, changes, comparisons.
The single biggest Task 1 upgrade: a clear Overview sentence ("Overall, it is clear that … while …"). Many Band 5–6 scripts describe data but never state the big picture. The overview is the first thing the examiner looks for.
🇹🇷 Process diagrams demand the passive ("the beans are roasted") — under-used by Turkish speakers. Drill passive transformations until they're automatic.

Writing Task 2 40 min · 250+ words (aim 280–320)

Types: Opinion · Discussion · Problem/Solution · Advantages/Disadvantages · Direct Question. Criteria: TR · CC · LR · GRA. Worth 2/3 of Writing.

The four-paragraph engine

  1. Intro (40–60w): background sentence + clear thesis (your position).
  2. Body 1 (80–100w): topic sentence → reason → example → link to thesis.
  3. Body 2 (80–100w): second idea or counter-argument, same shape.
  4. Conclusion (40–50w): restate thesis in new words + summary.
What lifts Band 5 → 7 fastest here: (1) answer every part of the question with a position held all the way through (Task Response); (2) one clear idea per paragraph (Coherence); (3) more error-free sentences (Grammar). Fancy vocabulary comes after these — not before.
🇹🇷 Avoid the calque opener "In today's modern world…" and the connector "In addition to this situation…". Use Furthermore / Moreover. And drop articles less — "the governments" → "governments" for general statements.

Daily Exercises — Small, Spaced, Retrieval-First

Short daily retrieval beats one big weekly cram. Attach each to an existing habit so it actually happens.

Habit hook (do this): "After my morning coffee, I do 10 minutes of vocab retrieval." Naming when and where roughly doubles follow-through. Rule: never miss twice. One skipped day is noise.

The 15–30 minute daily menu (pick one)

Mon — Vocab retrieval

Cover yesterday's words. Produce each from its definition + write one sentence. Retrieval, not re-reading.

Tue — Reading set

One question set (mix 2–3 types). Then explain why each answer is right — elaboration locks it in.

Wed — Sentence surgery

Take 5 of your own error sentences; rewrite correctly. Focus this week's grammar point.

Thu — Listening micro

One Section, predict gaps first, check spelling of every answer.

Fri — Speaking aloud

Record one Part 1 answer with AREA or one Part 2 cue card. Listen back once.

Sat — Timed task

One Writing task under the clock (see Practice tab timers).

The spacing schedule (how memory actually works)

Every new word or rule learned on Day 1 is retrieved again on Day 2 → Day 4 → Day 8 → Day 16. A word is "known" only after ~5 successful spaced retrievals in different contexts — not after one lesson.

Mike says: "Ten honest minutes a day will beat a heroic three-hour Sunday every single time. Consistency compounds; intensity collapses."

Grammar — Targeted at Your Band Ceiling

For a Band 5 writer, grammar accuracy and range are usually the biggest brake. These are the structures that unlock higher GRA bands — and the Turkish-L1 errors that cap them. One structure at a time, then spaced re-practice.

Grammar–band map

StructureBand it helps unlockBand 5 version → Band 7 version
Articles (a/an/the/zero)5.5 → 6"Government should help nature" → "The government should protect the environment"
Complex sentences (subordination)6 → 7"Cars cause pollution. This is bad." → "Because cars generate emissions, which harm air quality, …"
Passive voice6 (esp. T1 process)"They roast the beans" → "The beans are roasted"
Relative clauses6 → 7"Students who study abroad…" / "a policy that reduces waste"
Conditionals6.5 → 7"If governments invested more, emissions would fall"
Perfect aspect6 → 6.5"Pollution increased" → "Pollution has risen sharply over the past decade"

This-month grammar tasks (rotate one per week)

  1. Article drill: take 10 of your own sentences; mark every noun a/an/the/zero. Re-test the same set in 8 days.
  2. Passive transformation: rewrite 8 active process sentences into the passive ("First, the data is collected…").
  3. Sentence combining: join 6 pairs of simple sentences using which / because / although / whereas.
  4. Tense timeline: match 6 sentences to a timeline; fix any present-simple-for-everything habit.
  5. Connector swap: replace heavy calque connectors with single-word academic ones (see below).
🇹🇷 The six recurring Turkish-L1 grammar errors (and the fix):
  • Missing article (no article system in Turkish) → "The + noun" practice.
  • Passive avoidance → passive drills on Task 1 processes.
  • Connector overuse ("In addition to this situation,") → single words: Furthermore,
  • Wrong preposition (postposition mapping) → preposition-collocation drills.
  • Subject repetition / drop → pronoun practice (English needs the subject).
  • Tense inconsistency → timeline-tense matching.
Frame: this is predictable L1 transfer, not a personal weakness — and it's exactly the kind of error that's trainable.

Reading — True / False / Not Given TFNG

Passage extract: "The common swift spends almost its entire life in flight, even sleeping on the wing. Researchers tracking the birds found that some individuals remained airborne for ten months without landing. The species feeds exclusively on airborne insects. Although swifts are often mistaken for swallows, the two belong to entirely different families."

1. Swifts can sleep while flying.   (T / F / NG)
2. Swifts are a type of swallow.   (T / F / NG)
3. Swifts live longer than swallows.   (T / F / NG)
1. TRUE — "even sleeping on the wing" = sleep while flying.
2. FALSE — they "belong to entirely different families"; directly contradicted.
3. NOT GIVEN — lifespan is never mentioned. (Tempting to guess, but the text is silent — that's NG, not F.)

Listening — Note Completion N/F/T

Tapescript: "Right, so the workshop runs on the fourteenth of March, starting at half past nine. It's held in the Maxwell Building, room 2B, and there's a materials fee of fifteen pounds payable on the day." (NO MORE THAN ONE WORD AND/OR A NUMBER)

2. Room: ___

1. 14th   2. 2B   3. 15
🇹🇷 Note "fifteen" not "fifty", and "half past nine" = 9:30. Spelling/number accuracy = easy marks.

Writing Task 2 — Plan one Opinion

Prompt: Some people believe university education should be free for all students. To what extent do you agree or disagree?

Start the 40-minute timer above. Plan a thesis + two body ideas before writing.

Thesis: Largely agree — free tuition widens access and benefits society, though it must be funded sustainably.
Body 1: Access & equality — cost currently bars capable low-income students; free education lets talent, not wealth, decide. Example: countries with free tuition show broader participation.
Body 2: Societal return — graduates pay more tax, innovate, staff key services; so the state recoups its investment.
Concession: It is costly — but a graduate tax or tiered funding addresses this without charging upfront fees.
Conclusion: Restate: benefits to access and society outweigh the cost, provided funding is well-designed.

Speaking — Part 2 cue card Long turn

Describe a skill you would like to learn. You should say: what the skill is · why you want to learn it · how you would learn it · and explain how it would change your life.

1 minute to plan (jot 2–3 words per bullet), then talk for 2 minutes. Record yourself.

Open: "I'd like to talk about learning to code, specifically Python, which is something I've wanted to do for a while."
Close: "Overall, I'd say this skill would genuinely reshape my career options, which is why I'm so keen to start."
Notice the relative clause ("which…") and the closing signpost ("Overall, I'd say…") — both push toward Band 7.

Reading — Matching Headings MH

Match a heading to each paragraph. More headings than paragraphs. Read first + last sentence only.

¶A: "The earliest coins appeared independently in several ancient civilisations…"
¶B: "Today, however, physical cash is rapidly being replaced by digital payment systems…"

Headings: i) The future of cashless payment · ii) Why coins lost value · iii) The origins of currency

¶A → iii (origins) · ¶B → i (cashless future). Heading ii is the distractor — never used.

Answer Templates

Scaffolds, not scripts. Use them to organise fast, then fill with your own ideas and accurate language.

Writing Task 1 — universal frame

Intro: "The [graph/chart/table/diagram] illustrates [what] [where/when]." (paraphrase — change 60%+)

Overview: "Overall, it is clear that [main trend 1], while [main trend 2]." (no numbers)

Body 1: "In [year], [subject] stood at / accounted for [figure], compared to [contrast]."

Body 2: "Between [X] and [Y], [subject] rose/fell [sharply/gradually] from [..] to [..]."

Trend upgrades: went up → rose sharply / climbed steadily; went down → declined significantly; stayed same → remained stable / plateaued; biggest → accounted for the largest proportion.

Writing Task 2 — by essay type

Opinion

Intro: position clear. Body 1: strongest argument FOR. Body 2: concede a counter → refute it. Conclusion: reaffirm.

Discussion (both views + opinion)

Intro: both views + your view. Body 1: view A. Body 2: view B. Conclusion: which is more convincing & why.

Problem / Solution

Body 1: 2–3 causes/problems. Body 2: solutions matched to them. Conclusion: overall statement.

Advantages / Disadvantages

Body 1: advantages + examples. Body 2: disadvantages + examples. Conclusion: balanced judgement.

Speaking frames

Part 1 — AREA: Answer → Reason → Example → Add. (~5–8 sentences.)

Part 2 — long turn: "I'd like to describe…" → bullet 1 → bullet 2 (most detail) → bullet 3 (why/feelings) → "Overall, I'd say…"

Part 3 — PEEL: Point → Explain → Example → Link. Or concession: "While it's true that…, I'd argue that… because…"

Band 7 discourse markers: "From my perspective…", "Having said that…", "What's more…", "Admittedly…", "It's possible that…"

🇹🇷 Template warning: memorised templates help structure, but examiners penalise memorised content. Keep the frame, make every idea and example your own.

Skill-specific traps

Reading

Choosing FALSE when it's NOT GIVEN · hunting exact words instead of paraphrases · over-translating (kills speed).

Listening

Waiting for the exact word (it's paraphrased) · thirty/thirteen & 15th/50th · spelling errors on names/places · leaving gaps blank.

Writing T1

No overview sentence · putting numbers in the overview · describing every data point instead of selecting the key ones.

Writing T2

Not answering all parts · position drifting mid-essay · "In today's modern world…" opener · listing ideas without developing one.

Speaking

One-word Part 1 answers · jumping around the cue card · over-formal register · filler "yani/I mean" · freezing on silence (use a thinking phrase).
Mike says: "An error isn't a verdict — it's information. Each one tells us exactly what to practise next. We fix two or three at a time, never twenty. That's how you actually improve without drowning."

Band-Up Tips — What Moves You One Band

Each tip is the highest-leverage change for that skill. Master the lower band's move before reaching for the next.

Listening 5 → 6 → 7

  • →6: Predict word-type in the reading time; stop missing easy gaps.
  • →6.5: Train parallel expression — hear the idea, not the word.
  • →7: Master Section 3–4 signposting; never leave blanks; flawless spelling.

Reading 5 → 6 → 7

  • →6: Time discipline — 90-second rule, skip and return.
  • →6.5: Nail TFNG vs NG logic and writer's-opinion (YNG) questions.
  • →7: Matching Headings via main-idea-only reading; resist word-match traps.

Writing 5 → 6 → 7

  • →6: Answer all parts; clear 4-paragraph structure; T1 overview sentence.
  • →6.5: Hold one position throughout; one idea per paragraph; more error-free sentences.
  • →7: A variety of complex structures used accurately + precise, less-common vocabulary used naturally.

Speaking 5 → 6 → 7

  • →6: Extend with AREA/PEEL; stop one-word answers.
  • →6.5: Range of connectives; willing to speak at length; self-correct smoothly.
  • →7: Speak at length without effort; complex structures; less-common vocab; clear pronunciation.
The Band 7 throughline: across all four skills, Band 7 = "a range / variety of complex features used accurately, with frequent error-free output." Range and accuracy together — not one at the cost of the other. That's why we build accuracy first, then stretch range on top of it.
🇹🇷 Biggest single band-lifter for you: article + tense + passive accuracy. These three are the slow-burn GRA features — drilling them with spaced practice over months is what cracks the 5.5/6.0 ceiling.
